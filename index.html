Complete Buying Guide — What to Know Before You Buy

Sizing Your Shed: Calculate Before You Buy

Measure your intended contents before choosing a shed size. A standard lawnmower needs about 3x5 feet of floor space. A riding mower needs 5x7 feet. Add 18 inches of aisle space for any contents you'll need to access regularly. Most people who buy a 6x4 shed wish they'd bought a 8x6 within two years. When in doubt, go one size up — you'll fill it.

Steel vs. Resin vs. Wood — The Real Trade-offs

Steel: cheapest, strongest, but can dent and rust if galvanizing fails. Resin (PVC/polypropylene): maintenance-free, won't rust, but can fade and become brittle in extreme UV. Wood: most attractive, good insulation, but requires annual maintenance (staining/sealing) and is susceptible to rot and insects without treatment. Resin is the best low-maintenance choice for most homeowners.

Foundation Requirements — Don't Skip This

Every shed needs a level, stable base. Your options: pressure-treated wood skids (most common), concrete slab (most permanent), gravel pad with landscape fabric (good drainage), or interlocking deck blocks. Most sheds don't include a floor — you're building it on a foundation. An unlevel shed causes door alignment problems and structural stress. Invest time in a level foundation.

Anchoring Against Wind

An unanchored shed can be displaced or destroyed by high winds. Most kit sheds include or recommend anchor kits for attaching to a concrete foundation, or earth auger anchors for ground attachment. In hurricane or tornado-prone areas, proper anchoring is not optional — check local building codes for requirements in your jurisdiction.

HOA Approval Before You Buy

Many HOA communities have restrictions on shed size, material, color, placement (setback from property lines), and whether a permit is required. Check your CC&Rs and contact your HOA before purchasing any shed — getting approval for a non-compliant structure after installation is far more painful than verifying requirements upfront.

Organization Inside the Shed

A shed without internal organization becomes a pile of stuff — which defeats the purpose. Plan vertical storage before the shed is delivered: wall-mount tool holders, shelving units, overhead storage for seasonal items. The most effective sheds have 80% of storage on the walls and ceiling, leaving the floor clear for equipment like mowers and wheelbarrows.

Frequently Asked Questions

Backyard Storage Buildings & Outdoor Sheds — Your Questions Answered

What size backyard shed do I actually need?

Inventory what you need to store before deciding on size. Garden tools, a lawnmower, and seasonal decorations typically need a 6x8 to 8x10 shed. Add a riding mower, ATV, or extensive workshop equipment and you're looking at 10x12 or larger. Most people underestimate their storage needs — size up if you're between sizes.

How long do outdoor storage sheds last?

Quality resin sheds from Suncast, Keter, and Lifetime last 15–25 years with minimal maintenance. Steel sheds with good galvanizing last 10–20 years. Cheap imported steel sheds may show rust within 3–5 years in wet climates. Wood sheds, properly maintained, can last 30+ years. Brand and material quality matter more than price.

Do I need a permit for a backyard shed?

Permit requirements vary by municipality. Most jurisdictions require permits for sheds over 120–200 square feet or over a certain height. Smaller sheds are typically exempt. Check with your local building department and HOA before purchasing. Unpermitted structures can cause issues when selling your home.

What's the best foundation for a backyard shed?

A gravel pad with landscape fabric provides excellent drainage and is DIY-friendly. Concrete slab is the most permanent and level option, ideal for larger sheds or those used as workshops. Pressure-treated wood skids work for smaller sheds and allow future relocation. All options work — choose based on your budget, shed size, and permanence preference.

Can I insulate a backyard shed?

Yes — fiberglass batt insulation between wall studs (on wood or metal-framed sheds) dramatically improves temperature regulation for workshop use. Resin sheds are more difficult to insulate due to their panel construction. For year-round use or delicate storage (paint, batteries, electronics), insulation is worth the investment.

How do I secure a backyard shed from theft?

Upgrade the standard hasp to a quality padlock with a hardened shackle (Master Lock or similar). Add a hasp that mounts through the door frame with carriage bolts and backing plates — standard screw-mounted hasps can be pried off quickly. Interior storage of high-value items (power tools, bikes) with a secondary security device (bike lock through frame) adds another layer.

What's the best shed for a small yard or townhouse?

The Rubbermaid 5x4 and similar compact resin sheds are designed for space-constrained situations. They fit in side yards, against fences, and in some cases on deck surfaces (check weight load capacity). For very small spaces, a vertical storage unit or a wall-mounted weatherproof cabinet may be more practical than a ground-level shed.

Can I put a shed on grass?

Placing a shed directly on grass is not recommended — the grass dies and the moisture accelerates frame corrosion and potential floor rot. At minimum, remove the grass and lay landscape fabric under gravel or pavers. A gravel pad 4–6 inches deep provides good drainage and a stable base without the permanence of a concrete slab.

What's the best shed for storing a riding lawnmower?

You need at least a 10x10 shed for a standard riding mower with room to maneuver. The Keter Manor 6x8 double-door design handles some riding mowers, but check your specific mower's dimensions against the door opening width first. Most riding mowers need a 5-foot door width for comfortable access.

How do I keep pests out of a backyard shed?

Seal all gaps at the base, around door frames, and around any utility penetrations with steel wool followed by expanding foam — rodents chew through foam alone. Store seed, bird food, and pet food in metal containers with tight-fitting lids. Keep the shed floor clear of debris where insects and small animals nest. Avoid wood-to-soil contact on wood-framed sheds.
